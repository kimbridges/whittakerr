# Background: IBP Desert Biome study (Kim as Asst. Director)
_Captured 2026-05-15._

## Context

Kim shared this in the example-ideas discussion. He was
Asst. Director of the Desert Biome study, one of the US
International Biological Program (IBP) Biome programs of
the early 1970s. The Desert Biome program was among the
larger of the five US/IBP biome programs.

By his recollection, the Desert Biome study had roughly
five major study sites, one in each desert type. The
Grassland Biome program was also large, possibly with fewer
sites (his memory is fuzzy on exact site counts).

## Why this matters for whittakerr

This is a second pillar of Kim's authorial authority on the
biome concept, parallel to (and roughly contemporaneous with)
his Island Ecosystems work documented in
`background/island_ecosystems_context.md`. Two roles as
Asst. Director of major NSF-funded biome programs is not
background — it is a primary qualification.

The Desert Biome work has specific pedagogical value for
this project:

1. **The studies were explicitly *biome* studies.** Plotting
   their sites on the Whittaker biome diagram is
   self-referential in a useful way — biome research history
   meeting biome classification. The IBP era's institutional
   choice of biomes (Eastern Deciduous Forest, Grassland,
   Desert, Coniferous Forest, Tundra) maps onto Whittaker's
   polygons in a way the reader can see.

2. **Five sites within one biome category** reveal intra-
   biome heterogeneity. The Desert biome polygon on
   Whittaker's diagram is one shape, but the five IBP
   Desert Biome study sites occupy different positions
   within and around it. Plotting them shows that "desert"
   is not a uniform place — the categorical scheme glosses
   variation that field ecology was set up to measure. This
   makes Thread 7 (edges as locus of insight) and Thread 9
   (diagram-as-system) concrete: the polygon is a
   pragmatically drawn shape, not a discovery; the sites
   inside reveal what the shape simplifies.

3. **Authorial voice on the IBP era.** Kim can speak to the
   institutional context of biome research with first-hand
   authority — what the programs were trying to do, what
   they found, how the biome concept worked as an
   organizing frame for large-scale NSF investment in
   ecology.

## The US/IBP Biome Programs (for context)

The US International Biological Program ran roughly 1968-1974
(with publication continuing through the late 1970s). Its
biome-focused programs:

- **Eastern Deciduous Forest Biome** — Oak Ridge area
- **Grassland Biome** — Pawnee site, Colorado State University
- **Desert Biome** — Utah State University, multiple sites
  (Kim's program as Asst. Director)
- **Coniferous Forest Biome** — Pacific Northwest
- **Tundra Biome** — Alaska / Barrow

Each program produced syntheses, papers, and (in some cases)
edited volumes. The Desert Biome program's synthesis output
should be locatable; the specific sites and a citation chain
should be assemblable.

## David Goodall as PhD advisor (and Desert Biome Director)

Captured 2026-05-15 from Kim's mention; substantially
elevated 2026-05-15 (same session) when Kim revealed:
"David Goodall was my PhD advisor."

David Goodall (1914–2018) was Kim's PhD advisor, and later
the Director of the US/IBP Desert Biome program with Kim as
Asst. Director. The advisor relationship preceded and
contextualized the program one — Goodall trained Kim, and
then they worked together at scale on a major NSF undertaking.

Goodall was an Australian-born plant ecologist, pioneer of
statistical methods in vegetation analysis (including
ordination techniques and quantitative classification), and
a prolific publisher into his second century. He died at 104
in 2018, remaining intellectually active to the end.

Kim's specific observation about Goodall's trajectory:
after running the Desert Biome program (an explicitly
biome-scale undertaking), Goodall migrated his research
focus to ecosystem-scale work. Kim's framing: "the scale
to where David Goodall migrated after running the Desert
Biome." This trajectory — a researcher who has run a
biome-scale program moving to ecosystem-scale — is the
worked example for the new Scale chapter
(`chapter_scale_ideas.md` Thread 6). A researcher who has
felt the limits of biome-scale from inside can make the
scale choice explicit in subsequent work.

**The PhD-advisor fact changes how this example reads.**
Kim's witness on Goodall's trajectory is not reportorial
("a researcher I worked under"); it is the witness of the
doctoral student who watched the advisor recalibrate across
scales over time. The intellectual descent is formal and
direct. When the Scale chapter draws on this example, it
does so from a position of unusually close authority — the
student observing the teacher's later evolution. Worth
preserving that framing in the eventual prose rather than
flattening it into "I worked with him."

## Kim's ecology lineage

Three major figures in 20th-century ecology, three direct
relationships, with two of them foundational:

- **Robert H. Whittaker** — teacher at UC Irvine, late
  1960s. Kim attended Whittaker's 1969 five-kingdoms talk
  in person (captured in the History chapter draft).
  Whittaker the gradient ecologist, the diagram author.
- **David Goodall** — **PhD advisor**, then Director of
  the US/IBP Desert Biome program with Kim as Asst.
  Director (early 1970s). Goodall the statistical-
  vegetation methodologist. The formal intellectual
  descent.
- **Dieter Mueller-Dombois** — **lab partner for many
  years**, then Principal Investigator of the Hawaii
  Island Ecosystems IRP with Kim as Asst. Director.
  Co-author of *Aims and Methods of Vegetation Analysis*
  (with Heinz Ellenberg, 1974), a landmark methods
  reference. Long continuous working contact, not just
  project collaboration. (See updated
  `island_ecosystems_context.md`.)

The lineage isn't just "knew three eminent people." It's
the dissertation supervisor (Goodall), the long-time lab
partner (Mueller-Dombois), and a formative teacher
(Whittaker) — three different kinds of relationship,
covering three central figures of 20th-century vegetation
ecology. This is an unusually direct vantage point on the
history the whittakerr document discusses. The authorial
authority isn't borrowed; it's inherited and earned at the
daily-conversation level.

Worth promoting this lineage into proj_whittakerr.md main
fields when the Authorial Background section gets added
(still deferred, but the case is now strong enough that
deferral is harder to justify).
