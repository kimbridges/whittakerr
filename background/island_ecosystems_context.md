# Background: Island Ecosystems and the Mauna Loa Transect
_Captured 2026-05-13 (evening, closing note of the session)._

## Context

Kim's brief historical note at the end of a long
design-capture session. He was Assistant Director of the
Island Ecosystem project, an NSF-funded study on community
distributions on an island mountain (Mauna Loa). The project
explicitly tested Whittaker's hypothesis of individual species
distributions, on a smoother, longer mountain gradient than
Whittaker's own Smoky Mountains work could provide. The
research was published as a book titled "Island Ecosystems."
Kilauea volcano was the lower anchor point of the transect.
Kim added the convergence as the note landed: "Right now, on
my TV, the lower anchor point of our transect (Kilauea
volcano) can be seen erupting. Isn't it amazing how the
threads of one's life intertwine?"

## Dieter Mueller-Dombois — long-time lab partner

Added 2026-05-15 from Kim's later clarification.
Mueller-Dombois was Principal Investigator of the Hawaii
Island Ecosystems IRP, and **he and Kim shared a lab for
many years.** This is a substantially deeper relationship
than the "PI / Asst. Director" project framing suggested.
Long-term shared-lab partnership is intellectual
collaboration at the daily-conversation level, not just
working relationship.

Mueller-Dombois is the co-author (with Heinz Ellenberg,
1974) of *Aims and Methods of Vegetation Analysis*, one
of the genuine landmark methods references in vegetation
science — the standard work for the European-tradition
(Braun-Blanquet) approach to community-scale vegetation
analysis for two generations. Kim's intimate familiarity
with this volume is a function of the lab partnership;
the book is part of the daily intellectual context.

The European-tradition ordination methodology described
in Mueller-Dombois & Ellenberg 1974 is the same family of
techniques Kim is currently re-engineering (see "Adjacent
work" below). This places vegetation analysis methodology
at multiple scales — biome-scale (Whittaker), ecosystem-
scale (Island Ecosystems IRP), and community-scale (the
Mueller-Dombois ordination work) — all within Kim's direct
working history. The full scale range from Thread 10 of
`design_classification_to_mapping.md` is autobiographically
present.

## Adjacent work — Ceska/Roemer ordination re-engineering (2026)

Captured 2026-05-15 as a contextual note. Kim is currently

implements the computer-based version of the
European-tradition ordination methodology Mueller-Dombois &
Ellenberg describe — the hand calculation is impractical;
the software made it accessible; the software has aged out

demonstrates Kim's current active engagement at

## What this means for whittakerr

Kim is not building a teaching document about someone else's
classification scheme. He is an author who tested the
underlying hypothesis of that scheme with NSF funding, on a
superior mountain gradient, and published the synthesis. The
authority the project's prose carries is therefore not
secondhand — it is first-person research-grounded testimony.
This shifts what kind of document whittakerr is.

### For the History chapter (Ch 1, drafted)

The current draft already incorporates Kim's first-person
voice on knowing Whittaker at UC Irvine and attending his
1969 five-kingdoms talk. The Mauna Loa work deepens the
personal arc substantially. A possible new narrative spine
for the chapter:

1. Whittaker the teacher — UC Irvine, late 1960s. Already
   in the draft.
2. Whittaker's hypothesis on individualistic species
   distributions — already established in the draft via the
   Smoky Mountains gradient work.
3. Testing that hypothesis on Mauna Loa — the Island
   Ecosystems project, NSF-funded, Kim as Assistant Director.
   A long smooth slope as a cleaner gradient test than the
   Smokies could provide.
4. The synthesis — the published Island Ecosystems book.
5. The present project — whittakerr as a tool whose author
   has tested the foundational hypothesis the tool's biome
   classification rests on.

This is a stronger narrative spine than the current draft
has. Whether to fold it into the existing History chapter or
add a dedicated section ("Testing Whittaker on Mauna Loa" or
similar) is a decision for the next drafting session.

### For the "What Is a Biome?" chapter (Ch 2, ideation)

The gradient-ecologist-who-drew-categories thread in the
chapter's closing turn (in
`chapter_what_is_a_biome_ideas.md`) gains specific historical
grounding. The Island Ecosystems work IS a test of the
gradient view that the categorical Whittaker diagram softens.
Kim was part of the work that demonstrated the continuum

### For the Hawaii example

The chapter ideation file tentatively floated a Hawaii
example — elevational biome stacking on a single island. The
Mauna Loa transect is the version of that example with
actual research behind it. This may become the worked example
for the chapter, or for the document as a whole, replacing
or supplementing the Botanical Gardens example.

### For the toolkit

The transect data — community composition along the Mauna
Loa elevational gradient — is the kind of dataset the
whittakerr tools can analyze. Concrete analyses the toolkit
enables: where do the transect sample sites fall on the
Whittaker diagram? Do their measured biome identities match
the climate-predicted biome from `name_biome()`? How do the
transect's individual species distributions compare to the
biome envelope's prediction (continuous individualistic
response versus discrete-category prediction)? These are
real analyses with data Kim has direct authority over.

### For primary sources

The Island Ecosystems book is a primary source for the
document. Citations from it carry weight that secondary
literature citations cannot. Worth queuing as a primary
reference; an appendix-as-references section in the document
becomes more substantive if it includes Kim's own work as
ground-truth lineage.
