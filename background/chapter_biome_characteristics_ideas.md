## The reframing — from "area" to "functional structure"

The original outline thought of a biome's characteristics as
quantitative attributes: area first, then "other
characteristics" left undecided. That framing is the source
of the "conceptually unsettled" status. Two observations
resolve it.

First, the quantitative-area material is already spent.
`biome_composition()` returns each biome's area and
percentage share, and its first worked appearance is in the
Retrieving Biome Information chapter, where it gives a
retrieved point its regional context. Area as a biome
characteristic has therefore already been delivered. This
chapter does not need to repeat it.

Second, that frees Biome Characteristics to be the chapter
the McMahon story wants it to be: the chapter on a biome's
**functional structure**. The defining characteristic of a
biome is not how much of it there is, and not the list of
species in it. It is the roster of ecological roles the
biome supports, and the predictability with which those
roles get filled. That is a characteristic of a biome in
the deepest sense, and it is what this chapter is about.

This also gives the chapter a clear relationship to "What Is
a Biome?". That chapter established function-not-identity as
the *definition* of a biome — life forms over taxonomy, the
Amazon / Congo / Borneo convergence. Biome Characteristics
takes the same principle and makes it a *method*: it shows
the functional view doing work, predicting, and being
verified in the field. Definition there; method here.

## Thread 1 — The MacMahon desert small-mammal story

Kim's account, brought 2026-05-22, as the chapter's opening
and anchor. Preserved here close to verbatim; Kim flagged
that "I'm not sure if this is expressed quite right" and
that the story is true and was "one of my most important
lessons as I acquired ecological skills."

The story. Jim MacMahon was studying small mammals in the
US/IBP Desert Biome research program. Field sites sent him
lists of the species they had captured during inventory.
Studying the lists, MacMahon worked out that the small
mammals fell into about a dozen functional groups,
distinguished by what they ate and how they lived — seed
eaters sorted by the size of seed they handled, omnivores,
and others. A dozen is more than a handful but still few
enough to hold in mind. (Kim's first telling said "not too
many categories"; on a second pass he recalled the number as
roughly a dozen, which is the figure to use.) The species
filled the categories neatly, with two exceptions. One
category was missing from
an Arizona site. MacMahon called the site director and
asked. The answer: "We get it sometimes; we just didn't
think it was important." Hole filled — a sampling gap, not a
real absence. The second hole was at a New Mexico site.
Another phone call. This time a long pause, then: "We found
it last night for the first time. How did you know?"

Why the two holes matter, and matter differently. This is
the part Kim was right to be careful about. The two
exceptions are not the same kind of exception, and naming
the difference makes the story sharper rather than messier:

- **The Arizona hole was a verification of the observer.**
  The functional model said a role should be occupied; the
  field crew, focused on identifying species, had recorded
  the occupant only intermittently and judged it
  unimportant. The model caught a gap in the *sampling*,
  not in the *desert*.
- **The New Mexico hole was a verification of the model.**
  The role was genuinely unfilled in the record, and the
  functional model predicted an occupant anyway. The
  occupant was found — the night before the call. The model
  knew something the species-focused field record did not
  yet contain. "How did you know?" is the chapter's
  intellectual peak.

Together the two holes show the functional view doing two
jobs: auditing the completeness of observation, and making a
genuine, falsifiable, and confirmed prediction.

Kim's takeaway, verbatim and to be preserved: it "takes
skill to view the world functionally as you're tempted
(sometimes even forced) to see organisms at the species
level." Had MacMahon worked at the species level, the
functional structure of the desert — and its predictability
— would have been invisible. The story is a piece of
first-person witness, consistent with how the document
already uses Kim's career (the IBP weather-station story in
the Preface, the Goodall material in the Scale chapter).

## Thread 2 — Functional structure as the biome's characteristic

The general claim the story carries. A biome has a
characteristic functional structure: a roster of ecological
roles (guilds, life forms, functional groups) that its
climate and physiognomy support. The roster is short, it is
describable, and — this is the load-bearing point — it is
*predictable*. An unfilled role is an anomaly that asks for
an explanation: either the observation is incomplete (the
Arizona case) or a discovery is waiting (the New Mexico
case).

This is what it means to say two places "are the same
biome." It does not mean they share species. It means they
present the same roster of roles. A desert in Arizona and a
desert in New Mexico are the same biome because the same
functional slots are there to be filled, even when different
species fill them.

The chapter should make the roster concrete with MacMahon's
own categories — seed-eater size classes, omnivores, and so
on — kept at the dozen-or-so grain MacMahon used. The number
itself is worth dwelling on. A dozen is a deliberate choice
of grain: not two or three, which would lump distinct ways
of making a living into one bin, and not fifty, which would
be a species list again with the structure lost. Choosing
how finely to divide the fauna into functional groups is the
same kind of judgment the Whittaker diagram makes in
settling on nine biomes. This rhymes directly with "Why nine
biomes?" in What Is a Biome? (the bias-variance and
legibility tradeoff): MacMahon's dozen functional groups and
Whittaker's nine biomes are the same decision — how finely
to divide before division stops helping — made at two levels
of one hierarchy. Kim flagged this explicitly: the dozen
"further develops the idea of how many groups you establish
when you're dividing things up."

## Thread 3 — Convergent evolution, and the diagram as its instrument

Convergent evolution is the mechanism. Different lineages,
under the same selective pressures, arrive at the same body
plans and the same ecological roles. It is why the roster of
roles is portable: why a desert's functional structure looks
the same on different continents even though no species is
shared.

A precision point worth keeping straight in the draft.
MacMahon's two sites were both North American deserts, both
Desert Biome sites. The completeness of the roster *within*
a single biome region is mostly a community-assembly result:
an unoccupied role is an opportunity, and opportunities tend
to get taken. Convergent evolution is the *cross-region*,
cross-continent part of the story — the reason the Sonoran
and the Karoo and the Gobi present recognizably the same
functional cast with entirely separate evolutionary
histories. The chapter should use both ideas but not blur
them: assembly fills the roster locally; convergence makes
the roster the same everywhere.

The payoff, and a candidate chapter close. The Whittaker
diagram works — the same temperature-precipitation point
returns the same biome anywhere on Earth — *only because*
convergent evolution makes function track climate rather
than ancestry. If biomes were defined by their taxa, a
classification in climate space would be impossible: taxa
are contingent on biogeographic history, on which continent
a lineage happened to arise and where it could disperse. The
diagram is, in effect, a convergent-evolution instrument. It
can be drawn at all because the thing it classifies —
functional structure — is the thing convergence makes
universal. This ties Biome Characteristics back to the whole
document.

## Thread 4 — The toolkit's honest role

The whittakerr tools classify climate into a biome. They do
not, and cannot, measure functional structure: WorldClim
carries temperature and precipitation, not functional-trait
or community data. So this chapter is, like Transitions, a
discussion chapter more than a code chapter.

The tool's contribution is real but indirect, and the
chapter should state it plainly. The toolkit gets you to the
biome; the biome is what tells you which functional template
to expect. `name_biome()` returns "subtropical desert," and
the value of that answer is that it carries MacMahon's
roster with it — it says which roles should be present. The
classification is the entry point to the functional
prediction. That is the "adds value" Kim pointed to: biome
data used well sets up the functional question, even though
the tool does not answer it directly.

Whether the chapter carries any live code is an open
question (below). It may use `biome_composition()` once more,
or stay nearly all prose with the MacMahon story as its
spine.

## Thread 5 — Humans as biome inhabitants (Kim, 2026-05-22)

Kim's addition: it is fairly well known that people often
feel most comfortable living in the biome they were raised
in. "We are, after all, inhabitants of the biomes right
along with all the other organisms."

This belongs in Biome Characteristics because the chapter is
about who fills the roles, and humans fill roles too. For
most of the document the human stance has been the
observer's: someone at the diagram, placing a point, reading
a name. This thread turns that around. We are biome
organisms, with a climate niche like any other animal, and
the biome of childhood sets a quiet baseline for what
ordinary weather, light, and distance feel like. The
natal-biome comfort is the felt residue of that niche.

It connects to "The axes everyone knows" in What Is a
Biome?, which grounded the temperature and precipitation
axes in lived human experience. This thread extends that
point: not only have readers experienced T and P, they
occupy a region of the diagram themselves, and they got it
the way every organism gets its niche, by growing up inside
it. The claim should be stated as "widely felt" rather than
asserted as hard fact; it is a recognizable human experience,
not a measured law.

Placement: a short section near the end, after the
convergence material and before the close. It also sharpens
the handoff to Transitions: a shifting climate moves the
biome, so the biome a reader was raised in is not promised
to stay where they left it. The human stake makes the
Transitions chapter land harder.

  Biome Characteristics describes the functional roster of a

  edge, where rosters overlap, is the ecotone.
- **Verification spine** — the document's thesis is the move
  from subjective expert proposal to objective verification.
  The Whittaker diagram verifies that climate predicts
  biome; the functional view verifies that biome predicts
  community structure. MacMahon's prediction is the second
  link in that chain, tested and confirmed.
