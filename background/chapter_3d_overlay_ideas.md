## The Threads

### 1. The orographic verification logic

**The core argument.** The classifier consumes only annual
mean temperature (BIO1) and annual precipitation (BIO12).
Elevation is not an input. The Whittaker biome polygons are
defined in T-P space; terrain has no representation in the
classification machinery.

At the scale of the Hawaiian islands and similar
topographically dramatic regions, both T and P are dominated
by orography:

- **Temperature** follows the lapse rate. Hawaii's MAT drops
  roughly 6°C per 1000 m of elevation gain (the moist-
  adiabatic rate varies but is robustly altitude-driven). The
  summit of Mauna Kea sits in subarctic-coniferous territory
  in T-P space purely because of elevation.

- **Precipitation** is shaped by trade-wind orographic lift on
  the windward side and rain shadow on the leeward side. The
  Koolau windward face records over 7000 mm/year; the leeward
  Waianae lee records under 400 mm/year. Both totals are
  generated by the same trade winds interacting with the same
  topography in different orientations.

So the biome map derived from T and P alone should track
topographic features — ridgelines, saddles, lee/windward
divides, elevation bands — even though the classifier knows
nothing about elevation, aspect, or terrain shape.

**The verification claim.** When the 2D biome map is draped
over the 3D Earth surface, the biome boundaries should align
visibly with topography. Where they do, the physics is
visible: orography → (T, P) → classification → boundaries
that recapitulate the orography that drove the inputs. The
classifier is "right" in the sense that an independent
physical mechanism it doesn't know about shows through in its
output.

**Connection to verification-as-test-case-selection (Kim,
2026-05-16).** That principle was articulated for the choice
of test sites (Oahu had two independent verification paths:
Kim's direct knowledge and the Mueller-Dombois literature).
The orographic verification is structurally the same principle
at the rendering level: the 3D overlay provides an additional
verification path — physical mechanism — that operates on
every map produced, not just on test sites with named expert
witnesses.

This is verification by structure, not verification by
witness. It scales to maps where neither the operator nor any
literature can speak to local detail.

**Failure modes worth naming.** The argument has limits and
the chapter should be honest about them:

- **Microclimate exceptions.** Soil moisture, fog drip,
  catabatic winds, urban heat islands — none of these are in
  orographic lapse-rate logic. The classifier won't catch
  them; the 3D overlay won't reveal them either. Both 2D and
  3D representations miss the same things.
- **Resolution coupling.** The verification works to the
  extent that the climate data resolution captures the
  orographic variation. At 2.5' on Oahu, the dry pockets
  average away (Scale chapter prescription). The 3D overlay
  at coarse resolution would show a smoother climate than
  exists; the topographic detail visible in Google Earth
  outruns the climate raster. Worth flagging in the chapter.
- **Polygon edge effects in T-P space.** When a cell sits
  near a Whittaker polygon edge, small T or P changes flip
  the biome assignment. Topographic gradients can cross
  polygon edges in T-P space, producing biome transitions
  in the map that look like they should be smooth on the
  terrain but are stepped. This is correct behavior of a
  discrete classifier; the 3D overlay makes the stepped
  transitions geographically visible.

**The polygon-shadow-as-elevation-readout.** Both images
show a striking visual feature worth specific attention in
the chapter prose: a dark "shadow" thickness along the
polygon edges, visible most clearly where the edge crosses
steep terrain. This is not a rendering artifact — it's the
direct visual consequence of `altitudeMode = clampToGround`
plus `tessellate = 1`. The polygon edge sits at local
ground elevation along its entire length; viewed in tilted
3D the edge appears as a strip whose width is proportional
to the elevation change it spans. On a flat region the
shadow is near-zero; on a steep slope it widens
dramatically.

The shadow is a direct readout of how much elevation the
polygon edge crosses. The chapter can name this explicitly:
the reader who looks at these figures should see not just
"the polygon fits the terrain" but "the polygon edge IS the
terrain" in the sense that elevation change at the boundary
is visible as edge thickness. This is the orographic
argument made visually self-demonstrating — the rendering
itself displays the mechanism the chapter argues for.

### 2. Mismatches as discovery surfaces — moving down the ecological hierarchy

_Added 2026-05-17 (continued) from Kim's framing of how the
3D overlay's apparent failures are actually its most productive
output._

Thread 1's orographic verification handles the "biome map looks
right because of physics" case: T and P track topography, the
biome map derived from T-P alone produces boundaries that
recapitulate the terrain. When the 3D overlay shows this
alignment, the classification is verified.

This thread handles the complementary case: when the 3D overlay
shows mismatches — biome assignments that don't match the
ecology actually present at the place, or terrain features the
biome map misses or oversimplifies. Kim's claim:

> "Where we find mismatches (edge cases?), it is likely not a
> failure of the biome classification but the need to explore
> the ecology at a lower level on the hierarchy (i.e.,
> ecosystems). [...] these edge cases are where we learn a
> lot, such as fire impacts being as important as the T and P
> factors in a local situation."

**The hierarchy view.** The biome classifier operates at one
level of the ecological hierarchy. T and P are the inputs;
biome is the output. By design, the classifier cannot see
factors that operate one level down the hierarchy:

- **Fire regime.** Fire-maintained ecosystems persist outside
  what their T-P envelope would predict in the absence of
  fire. Canonical cases include African savanna (would be
  forest without fire), longleaf pine in the southeastern US,
  California chaparral, Australian sclerophyll communities,
  Hawaiian native grasslands. The climate envelope says one
  thing; the actual ecosystem says another; the difference is
  fire frequency.
- **Soil chemistry.** Serpentine soils host endemic
  communities that look nothing like the surrounding biome.
  Calcareous outcrops, saline flats, ultramafic substrates —
  all produce local ecosystems that diverge from the climate
  prediction.
- **Hydrology.** Riparian zones, wetlands, springs, seasonally
  flooded depressions — local water availability decouples
  the ecosystem from regional precipitation totals.
- **Disturbance history.** Recent logging, agricultural
  abandonment, grazing pressure, hurricane tracks — these
  produce ecosystem states that lag the climate envelope by
  decades or centuries.
- **Biotic interactions.** Beaver activity creates wetland
  systems; bark beetle outbreaks shift forest composition;
  invasive species displace native communities.

**The methodological move.** Apparent classification errors
become a research methodology. When the 3D overlay reveals a
mismatch between predicted biome and observed ecosystem:

- First ask: is this a calibration issue (climate raster
  resolution, polygon edge case in T-P space, GADM boundary
  artifact)? If so, the chapter's resolution-coupling
  discussion (Scale chapter forward-reference) applies.
- If it's not calibration, the mismatch points to an
  ecosystem-level factor operating locally. The mismatch is
  the SIGNAL, not the noise. The chapter can use mismatches
  as worked examples of "this is where you go look for fire
  history" or "this is where soil maps will tell you more
  than climate maps."

**The classification's incompleteness is the feature.** A
climate-based classifier that captured everything wouldn't
direct attention to the next level of the hierarchy. Because
the biome classifier is intentionally simple — T and P only —
its failures are diagnostic. Each failure points specifically
at a factor the classifier doesn't know about, and the spatial
pattern of failures suggests which factor.

This connects to Thread 7 of `chapter_mapping_ideas.md`
(edges as locus of insight) but at a different scale. Thread 7
is about edges between biomes within the classification system
(where T-P gradients cross polygon boundaries). This thread is
about edges between the classification system and the actual
ecology of a place (where T-P alone fails to predict the
ecosystem). Both are productive boundaries; both are where the
work happens.

**Connection to Kim's research lineage.** The IBP biome
programs that Kim served as Assistant Director (Desert and
Tropical) operated at the ecosystem level, not the biome
level. The biome scheme provides a coarse-grained map; the
IBP-era ecosystem studies filled in the finer-grained
processes. The 3D overlay's mismatches re-create that
relationship: the biome map is the first cut, the mismatches
identify where ecosystem-level investigation is warranted.

**Pedagogical use in the chapter.** The chapter can carry one
worked mismatch example as a parallel to the Oahu success
case.

A figure asset is already in hand:
`images/oregon_3d_eastern_high_desert.png` (see Thread 1's
screenshots section) is the setup figure for this thread.
The grassland/desert basins and woodland/shrubland ranges
visible in that image are precisely the locations where
the Oregon-specific mismatch cases enumerated in
`Whittakerr_3d.Rmd`'s "Mismatches worth investigating"
section apply: ponderosa fire ecology in the surrounding
forests, riparian gallery forests along the John Day and
Owyhee drainages, serpentine endemics in the Klamaths to
the west. The same image that demonstrates Thread 1's
verification claim in eastern Oregon doubles as the visual
launchpad for this thread's discussion of where the
verification's apparent limits become research-direction
markers.

Candidates for a deeper worked mismatch example, beyond the
Oregon setup case:

- **A fire-maintained system that the biome map misclassifies.**
  Southeastern US longleaf pine — climate envelope predicts
  temperate seasonal forest; actual ecosystem (where fire
  regime is intact) is open pine woodland with grass
  understory. The mismatch is direct evidence of fire's
  ecosystem-shaping role.
- **A serpentine endemism case.** California's serpentine
  outcrops within Mediterranean-climate biome assignments —
  the biome map says shrubland; the local ecosystem is
  serpentine chaparral or serpentine grassland depending on
  outcrop.
- **A hydrological case.** A desert biome assignment that
  contains a riparian gallery forest along a perennial
  stream. The map says desert; the ground truth is desert
  PLUS a thin green ribbon of forest the climate raster
  averaged away.

Each example follows the same arc: biome map predicts X;
3D overlay shows where the prediction sits; ecology at the
mismatch site requires a hierarchy-level-down explanation.

**Falsifiability implication.** This thread strengthens
Thread 1's falsifiability argument. A region with weak
orography (Iowa) shouldn't show topographic alignment in its
biome boundaries — that's Thread 1's negative test. A region
with strong fire history (longleaf pine belt) should show
biome-to-ecosystem mismatch — that's this thread's negative
test. Both tests demonstrate the verification logic is a
real mechanism, not a tautology, and that the chapter is
making falsifiable claims rather than just admiring its own
output.

### 3. KML export — the technical mechanism

Carried from `chapter_mapping_ideas.md` Thread 6 (2026-05-15)
with implementation-readiness updates.

Two implementation paths, both viable:

- **Raster GroundOverlay (KML).** Render the biome map as a
  georeferenced PNG, wrap in a KML GroundOverlay element
  pointing at the bounding-box coordinates. Simpler; the
  PNG carries the pixel-level color information directly.
  Trade-off: loses vector quality at zoom; transparency
  works well over terrain; biome colors visible against
  the landscape.

- **Vector KML polygons.** Write the classified polygons
  (the `biome_polygons` already produced by
  `smooth_biome_map()` or by raster-to-polygon conversion)
  directly as KML Placemarks with Polygon geometry and color
  fills via Style elements. Vector-quality at any zoom;
  each polygon needs explicit color encoding; more lines of
  code; more faithful at boundaries.

**Toolchain candidates in R:**

- `sf::st_write(..., driver = "KML")` — handles both
  geometries. Cleanest for the vector polygon path. Already
  a project dependency (used in `smooth_biome_map()`).
- `terra::writeVector(..., filetype = "KML")` — handles
  SpatVector directly. Possibly the natural fit given the
  existing pipeline produces `biome_polygons` as SpatVector.
- `plotKML` package — purpose-built but possibly stale; check
  for maintenance status before adopting.
- `leaflet` package — can export to GeoJSON; KML adjacent
  rather than direct.

**Style integration with the existing toolkit.** The vector
KML path inherits color information from the same Ricklefs
palette used by `plot_biome_map()`. The chapter can show that
the same biome map appears in three modes — diagram (T-P
space), gridded geographic (2D map), and 3D draped (Google
Earth) — all with consistent color identity. This is a
substantive contribution to teachability: the reader carries
one color vocabulary across all three views.

### 5. Worked examples — verification surfaces

Following the verification-as-test-case-selection principle
(Kim, 2026-05-16): example selection should prefer cases with
strong independent verification paths.

**Primary worked example: Oahu in 3D.**

The Oahu biome map already exists at 30 arcsec resolution
with the verified subtropical desert finding. Draping that
map over Google Earth's terrain gives:

- **Windward Koolau wall** — biome boundary should align
  with the ridgeline crest. The rain-shadow transition is
  abrupt across the ridge; the biome map should show that
  abruptness; Google Earth's terrain shows where the ridge
  actually is. Convergence verifies the orographic logic.
- **Leeward dry pockets at Kaena Point and Waianae lee** —
  the subtropical desert assignments should sit in
  topographically defensible locations (low elevation,
  leeward of the Waianae crest, exposed coastal). The
  geometry of the assignment relative to the ridgeline is
  visible only in 3D.
- **Central plain transition** — the Schofield-area lowland
  between the two ranges should show a different biome
  signature than the surrounding higher terrain. The
  elevation differential is modest; how it shows up in
  biome classification is a calibration check on the climate
  raster's vertical sensitivity.

Verification paths available for Oahu: (1) Kim's direct
knowledge, (2) Mueller-Dombois literature, (3) Google Earth
terrain itself. Three independent paths.

**Secondary worked example: Hawaii Island in 3D.**

Bigger canvas, more biome diversity, the Mauna Loa
elevational transect as research-historical anchor. Kim's
Island Ecosystems IRP research site sits on Mauna Loa; the
chapter can carry this as a worked example AND as a personal
connection (the IBP era research informs the modern toolkit
output).

Expected biome stack on Mauna Loa from sea level to summit
(from Mueller-Dombois work and standard Hawaiian ecology):
tropical seasonal forest (lower windward) → temperate
seasonal forest (mid-elevation) → woodland-shrubland
(upper elevation, drier) → subarctic-coniferous or tundra-
equivalent (alpine). The biome classifier should produce
something close to this stack from T and P alone; the 3D
overlay makes the elevational structure directly visible.

Verification paths: (1) Kim's research-site direct knowledge,
(2) published Hawaiian biome literature, (3) the elevational
stack itself as a known phenomenon, (4) Google Earth terrain.
Four independent paths — Hawaii Island is the strongest test
case for the orographic verification claim.

**Tertiary worked example or reference-only: Oregon in 3D.**

Already produced in 2D (gridded + smoothed). The Cascades
form a north-south barrier creating a sharp east-west
moisture gradient; the 3D overlay should show the biome
boundary tracking the Cascades crest. Different geometry
from Hawaii (continental scale, single mountain range,
different orientation) but same physical mechanism. Useful
as a "the verification logic works in non-island settings
too" example.

**Comparative example deferred:** showing the SAME 3D
verification on a topographically subtle region (e.g.,
Iowa, Florida) where the orographic effect is weak and the
biome map should NOT show topographic correlation. This is
methodologically the most interesting comparison — it
demonstrates that the verification logic is properly
falsifiable, not a tautology. But it adds chapter scope.
Defer the decision to drafting.

  less convincing than it is. Default to no exaggeration

  can adjust.
